Why Cells Matter in Pathology

Pathology literally means the study of suffering (Greek: pathos = suffering, logos = study). Virchow correctly stated that disease originates at the cellular level — disturbances in cells arise from alterations in molecules (genes, proteins, and other molecules).

🔑 Core Principle Understanding the cellular and molecular abnormalities that give rise to disease is the foundation of modern pathology.

Sequencing the Human Genome

The sequencing of the human genome at the beginning of the 21st century was a landmark achievement. It allowed us to analyze vast amounts of data about health and disease. Key revelations include:

  • Humans have only ~20,000 protein-coding genes — far fewer than expected.
  • With genomes 30-fold smaller, we are assembled from roughly 20,000 proteins.
  • Most of these proteins are recognizable across many organisms — what separates us from worms is how the genes are assembled and regulated.
  • More than 1/3 of the human genome is made of "jumping genes" (transposons).
📌 Key Insight The genome codes for about 20,000 proteins, yet more than 85% of the human genome is transcribed into RNA — meaning vast amounts of RNA have roles beyond protein coding.

Noncoding DNA

// Module 02 · Regulation Beyond Genes

What Is Noncoding DNA?

Only about 1.5% of our DNA directly codes for proteins. The remaining ~98.5% was once dismissed as "junk DNA" but is now known to be functionally critical for regulating gene expression and maintaining chromosome structure.

PromotersEnhancersIntrons5'-UTR3'-UTRTelomeresCentromeres

SNPs — Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms

SNPs are single nucleotide variations at a given position in the genome. Key facts:

  • Occur every 300 base pairs on average
  • 99.9% identical between two people
  • More than 6 million human SNPs identified
  • Can be in coding or regulatory regions
  • Most SNPs are "neutral" variants
⚠️ Important SNPs don't "explain" disease inheritance alone — they identify risk, not cause.

CNVs — Copy Number Variations

CNVs are segments of DNA (1,000–1,000,000+ base pairs) that are deleted, duplicated, or rearranged:

  • Can be simple duplications or complex rearrangements
  • Responsible for millions of base pairs of sequence difference between individuals
  • Approximately 50% of CNVs involve gene-coding sequences
  • A large proportion of human phenotypic diversity is CNV-based

Structure of a Gene (Key Anatomy)

  • Promoter — Upstream region where RNA polymerase binds to start transcription
  • Exons — Protein-coding sequences that are retained in mature mRNA
  • Introns — Non-coding sequences spliced out of pre-mRNA
  • 5'-UTR — Untranslated region; regulates translation efficiency
  • 3'-UTR — Untranslated region; important for mRNA stability and miRNA binding
  • Open Reading Frame (ORF) — Sequence translated into protein
🚨 Clinical Link Alterations in DNA sequence (SNPs, CNVs) cannot by themselves explain phenotypic differences or classic Mendelian genetic traits — epigenetic changes must also be considered.

Histone Organization & Epigenetics

// Module 03 · Chromatin Remodeling

Chromatin: DNA Packaging

DNA doesn't float freely — it's wound around proteins called histones. This complex is called chromatin.

147
Base Pairs per Nucleosome
8
Histone Proteins (H2A, H2B, H3, H4 ×2)

Nucleosomes are octamers of histone proteins. DNA wraps around these beads, and the entire structure is joined by short linker DNA and a linker histone (H1).

Heterochromatin (Inactive)

Densely packed, transcriptionally inactive chromatin. Genes within are silenced. Associated with:

  • DNA methylation
  • Histone deacetylation
  • Gene silencing

Euchromatin (Active)

Dispersed, transcriptionally active chromatin. Genes within are expressed. Associated with:

  • Histone acetylation
  • Histone methylation (at specific residues)
  • DNA accessibility for RNA polymerase

Epigenetic Modifications — How Gene Expression Is Controlled

Cell-type-specific differences in gene expression are regulated by epigenetic modifications — changes that don't alter DNA sequence but control whether genes are on or off:

  • DNA Methylation — Methyl groups added to cytosines; generally condenses chromatin and silences gene expression. Associated with transcriptional inactivation.
  • Histone Acetylation — Loosens chromatin, promotes transcription (gene activation).
  • Histone Methylation — Effect depends on which residue is methylated; can activate or repress.
  • Histone Phosphorylation — Can influence chromatin structure and DNA repair responses.
🔑 Clinical Significance Epigenetic modifications are reversible — this is why HDAC inhibitors and demethylating agents are used as cancer therapy. Epigenetic drugs are already used in treatment of several forms of cancer.
⚠️ Disease Link Disruption of epigenetic marks (mutations in histone-modifying enzymes) is a hallmark of many cancers and developmental disorders. DNA methylation of specific genes can silence tumor suppressors.

miRNA & Long Noncoding RNA

// Module 04 · RNA-Based Gene Regulation

MicroRNAs (miRNA)

Short RNA sequences (~22 nucleotides) that function as post-transcriptional regulators:

  • Encoded by ~1,000 miRNA genes in humans
  • Transcribed as pre-miRNA, processed by Dicer into miRNA (~22 nt)
  • Bind RISC complex → bind to complementary 3'-UTR of target mRNAs
  • Result: mRNA cleavage (perfect match) or translation repression (imperfect match)
  • One miRNA can regulate hundreds of mRNAs
🔑 Key Fact miRNA regulation of gene expression is a fundamental and critical mechanism present in ALL eukaryotes.

Long Noncoding RNAs (lncRNA)

>200 nucleotides in length. Functions:

  • Gene activation (binding transcription factor complexes)
  • Gene suppression (acting as decoy RNA)
  • Promoting chromatin modification (methylation/acetylation)
  • Serving as scaffold for secondary protein complexes
  • Assembly of multisubunit complexes
📌 Notable Example XIST — the lncRNA responsible for X chromosome inactivation in females. XIST escapes X inactivation and acts in gene silencing on the same chromosome.

miRNA Mechanism: Step by Step

  • miRNA gene is transcribed in the nucleus → forms primary miRNA (pri-miRNA)
  • Pri-miRNA is processed within the nucleus → forms pre-miRNA (~70 nt hairpin)
  • Pre-miRNA is exported to the cytoplasm via export proteins
  • Cytoplasmic enzyme Dicer cuts the pre-miRNA → mature double-stranded miRNA (~22 nt)
  • miRNA is incorporated into the RISC complex (RNA-Induced Silencing Complex)
  • RISC + miRNA binds to target mRNA → silences gene expression
🚨 Disease Relevance miRNAs can function as oncogenes or tumor suppressors. Dysregulation is involved in atherosclerosis, cancer, and developmental disorders. miRNA-based tools are being explored as diagnostics and therapeutics.

Gene Editing (CRISPR-Cas9)

// Module 05 · Molecular Revolution

What Is CRISPR-Cas9?

CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) with Cas9 nuclease is a revolutionary gene-editing system adapted from bacterial immune defense mechanisms against bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria).

🔑 Origin Bacteria use CRISPR to sample the DNA of infecting agents, incorporating it into the host genome. CRISPRs are transcribed and processed into RNA that guides Cas9 to cleave viral DNA on re-exposure.

How CRISPR-Cas9 Works

  • Scientists design an artificial guide RNA (gRNA) complementary to a target DNA sequence
  • gRNA binds to Cas9 nuclease and directs it to the exact genomic location
  • Cas9 introduces a double-stranded DNA break at the target site
  • The cell repairs the break → insertions or deletions (gene knockout) OR
  • A donor DNA template is provided → precise gene correction via homologous recombination
📌 Real Beauty of CRISPR Entire genomes can be delivered using a single easy-to-make plasmid — compared to previous tools like NHEJ (harder) and ZFN (expensive and less precise).

Applications

  • Inserting specific sequences into genomes
  • Correcting mutations causing hereditary disease
  • Rapidly generating transgenic animals
  • Other rapidly advancing gene therapies

Limitations & Ethics

  • Off-target cuts remain a concern
  • Germline editing is deeply controversial
  • Delivery to target tissues is challenging
  • Justice concerns around access
⚠️ Ethical Debate CRISPR's ability to precisely edit the human germline — correcting heritable disease or potentially introducing "desirable" traits — has ignited vigorous ethical debate in the scientific and public sphere.

Cellular Housekeeping

// Module 06 · Organelle Function

Compartmentalization

Normal housekeeping functions are compartmentalized within membrane-bound intracellular organelles (the "little organs" of the cell). This allows potentially destructive enzymes or reactive metabolites to be concentrated without risking damage to other cellular constituents.

🟦 Rough ER (RER)

Ribosomes stud its surface. Functions:

  • Synthesis of secreted proteins
  • Synthesis of membrane proteins
  • Initial glycosylation of proteins
  • Disulfide bond formation
⚠️ Pathology ER stress → unfolded protein response (UPR) → if unresolved, triggers apoptosis.

🟨 Golgi Apparatus

The cell's "post office." Functions:

  • Protein modification (glycosylation)
  • Sorting and packaging proteins
  • Directing proteins to destinations (secretion, lysosomes, plasma membrane)

🔴 Lysosomes

Contain ~40 hydrolytic enzymes. Functions at acidic pH (≈4.5–5):

  • Digest macromolecules, old organelles, engulfed microbes
  • Intracellular digestion (heterophagy — external material)
  • Autophagy — degrading the cell's own components
Disease Lysosomal storage diseases (e.g., Gaucher's, Tay-Sachs) result from enzyme deficiencies.

🟣 Proteasomes

Cytosolic "disposal complexes." Functions:

  • Degrade misfolded or damaged proteins
  • Target proteins tagged with ubiquitin chains
  • Regulation of transcription factors (e.g., p53)
Clinical Link Proteasome inhibitors (e.g., bortezomib) are used in cancer treatment.

Autophagy vs. Heterophagy

  • Autophagy (Self-eating): Lysosomes fuse with autophagosomes to degrade the cell's own worn-out organelles. Important for cellular survival during starvation.
  • Heterophagy: Lysosomes fuse with phagosomes containing engulfed external material (e.g., bacteria, cellular debris).
  • Residual bodies: Indigestible material accumulates as lipofuscin pigment — a hallmark of aging cells.

Plasma Membrane

// Module 07 · Protection & Transport

Structure of the Plasma Membrane

Plasma membranes are fluid bilayers of amphipathic phospholipids with hydrophilic head groups and hydrophobic tails. Key features:

  • Cholesterol — provides mechanical stability and regulates fluidity
  • Glycolipids and glycoproteins — form the extracellular "sugar coat" (glycocalyx)
  • Lipid-linked proteins — anchored to outer or inner leaflet (e.g., via GPI anchors)
  • Transmembrane proteins — span the bilayer; carry out most membrane functions
🔑 Asymmetry Membrane lipids are distributed asymmetrically. Phosphatidylserine (normally inner leaflet) flipping to the outer surface signals apoptosis and activates clotting.

How Molecules Cross the Membrane

  • Passive Diffusion: Small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂, steroid hormones) cross freely. No energy needed.
  • Channel Proteins: Create hydrophilic pores — restricted by size and charge (e.g., aquaporins, ion channels). Rapid transport.
  • Carrier Proteins: Bind specific substances and undergo conformational changes; slower but more selective.
  • Endocytosis: Cell engulfs large molecules or particles into vesicles.
  • Exocytosis: Vesicles fuse with the plasma membrane to release contents extracellularly.

Clathrin-Mediated Endocytosis

Receptor-ligand complexes are internalized via clathrin-coated pits. Used for:

  • LDL receptor uptake (defective in hypercholesterolemia)
  • Iron-transferrin uptake
  • Growth factor internalization

Transcytosis

Material is transported across epithelial cells — from one side to the other — via vesicles. Important for:

  • IgG transport across placenta
  • Antibody transfer in breast milk
  • Drug delivery across the blood-brain barrier

Cytoskeleton

// Module 08 · Structural Framework

Three Major Cytoskeletal Proteins

The cytoskeleton allows cells to maintain shape, move organelles, divide, and migrate. Three major classes:

Actin Filaments

  • 5–9 nm thin filaments
  • Formed from globular actin (G-actin)
  • Control cell shape & movement
  • Form the contractile apparatus with myosin
  • Present at microvilli (surface projections)
Motor: Myosin

Intermediate Filaments

  • 8–10 nm diameter
  • Stable, non-dynamic
  • Provide structural support
  • Types: Keratins (epithelial), Vimentin (mesenchymal), Desmin (muscle), Neurofilaments (neurons)
Clinical Use Keratin expression patterns are used as histochemical markers to identify cell lineage in tumors.

Microtubules

  • 25 nm hollow tubes
  • Made of α-β tubulin dimers
  • Organize organelle position
  • Form mitotic spindle for cell division
  • Direct vesicle transport along cell
Drug Target Taxol and Vinca alkaloids (anticancer drugs) target microtubule polymerization/depolymerization.

Cell-Matrix & Cell-Cell Adhesions

  • Focal adhesions — Link actin cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix (ECM) via integrins
  • Desmosomes — Link intermediate filaments between adjacent epithelial cells; provide mechanical strength
  • Hemidesmosomes — Link intermediate filaments to the basement membrane (basal lamina)
  • Gap junctions — Connexin channels that allow ion/small molecule transfer between cells

Cell-Cell Junctions & Communication

// Module 09 · Social Cells

Three Major Junction Types

Cells interact and communicate through junctions that provide mechanical links and enable recognition of ligands and extracellular molecules:

🚫 Tight Junctions (Occluding)

Seal adjacent cells to form a continuous barrier restricting paracellular movement of ions and molecules. They:

  • Maintain epithelial polarity (apical vs basolateral)
  • Are formed by claudins and occludins (multiprotein complexes)
  • Are a high-resistance barrier to solute movement

⚓ Anchoring Junctions (Desmosomes)

Mechanically attach cells and connect cytoskeletons. Types:

  • Spot desmosomes — link intermediate filaments between cells; small and rivet-like
  • Belt desmosomes (adherens) — link actin filaments; run around cell perimeter
  • Hemidesmosomes — link cells to basal lamina
  • Focal adhesions — link actin to ECM via integrins

⚡ Gap Junctions

Gap junctions mediate the passage of chemical and electrical signals between cells. Made of connexins (transmembrane proteins). They:

  • Allow ions, small metabolites, and vitamins to pass directly
  • Allow calcium fluxes through myocardium (cardiac muscle = functional syncytium)
  • Range: 1.5–2 nm in diameter transmembrane channels
🔑 Pathology Loss of gap junction proteins is associated with certain inherited disorders, cardiac arrhythmias, and deafness.

Extracellular Matrix (ECM)

The ECM is a network of proteins and polysaccharides filling extracellular space. Major components:

  • Collagen — Most abundant protein in the body; provides tensile strength
  • Laminin — Cross-shaped glycoprotein; major component of basement membrane
  • Fibronectin — Connects cells to matrix; important in wound healing
  • Proteoglycans — Hydrophilic core proteins; create a "gel" that resists compression

Cellular Metabolism & Mitochondria

// Module 10 · Energy, Life & Death

The Mitochondrion: A Cell Within a Cell

Mitochondria evolved from ancestral symbiotic bacteria — which is why they have their own circular DNA (mtDNA), ribosomes, and double membrane. Key facts:

37
Genes in mtDNA
~1%
of total cellular protein
1700
Mitochondria per Hepatocyte
🔑 Maternal Inheritance mtDNA is inherited almost entirely maternally. Mitochondrial diseases can be X-linked, autosomal, or maternally inherited.

ATP Generation

  • Main site: inner mitochondrial membrane
  • Oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) — electron transport chain + ATP synthase
  • Glucose → pyruvate → acetyl-CoA → TCA cycle → NADH/FADH₂ → ETC → ATP

Non-ATP Roles

  • Intermediary metabolism (TCA intermediates)
  • Synthesis of certain macromolecules
  • Generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS)
  • Regulation of intracellular calcium
  • Cell death decisions (apoptosis)

⚠️ Warburg Effect — Cancer Metabolism

Rapidly growing cancer cells preferentially use aerobic glycolysis (glucose → lactic acid) even in the presence of oxygen — this is called the Warburg Effect:

  • Less efficient for ATP but provides biosynthetic precursors
  • Glutamine is also consumed to provide acetyl-CoA (lipids) and nucleotides
  • Basis for 18F-FDG PET scanning in oncology (cancer cells consume more glucose)

Mitochondria and Cell Death

Mitochondria are central regulators of apoptosis (programmed cell death):

  • Injury signals → mitochondrial permeability transition (membrane pores open)
  • Cytochrome C is released from the intermembrane space into the cytosol
  • Cytochrome C activates the apoptosome → activates caspases
  • Caspases execute cellular self-destruction — DNA fragmentation, membrane blebbing
🚨 Clinical Relevance BCL-2 family proteins govern the decision to release cytochrome C. BCL-2 is anti-apoptotic; BAX/BAK are pro-apoptotic. Many cancers overexpress BCL-2 to evade cell death.

Cellular Activation & Signal Transduction

// Module 11 · Communication Systems

Types of Cell Signaling by Distance

  • Autocrine — A cell responds to signals it secretes itself (e.g., tumors stimulate their own growth)
  • Paracrine — A cell affects nearby cells via secreted factors diffusing short distances (e.g., nitric oxide from endothelium → smooth muscle relaxation)
  • Synaptic — Activated neurons release neurotransmitters at a synapse
  • Endocrine — Hormones are released into blood and act on distant target organs

Signal Transduction: From Receptor to Nucleus

Regardless of the signal type, it is transmitted via a receptor and initiates a cascade of intracellular events. Main receptor classes:

1. Cell Surface Receptors Most hydrophilic ligands bind here. Three types: (1) Ion channel-coupled, (2) G-protein coupled (GPCRs), (3) Enzyme-linked (e.g., tyrosine kinase receptors).
2. Intracellular Receptors Lipid-soluble ligands (e.g., steroid hormones, thyroid hormone) diffuse into cell → bind nuclear receptors → directly regulate transcription.

🔑 Key Signaling Pathways

  • RAS/MAPK Pathway: Growth factor → receptor tyrosine kinase → RAS (activated by GTP exchange) → RAF → MEK → ERK → nucleus → gene expression (cell proliferation). RAS is mutated in ~30% of human cancers.
  • PI3K/AKT/mTOR Pathway: Growth factor → PI3K → PIP3 → AKT → mTOR → protein synthesis, cell survival. PTEN tumor suppressor inhibits this pathway.
  • Notch Pathway: Ligand (Delta/Jagged) on one cell binds Notch receptor on adjacent cell → releases intracellular domain → transcription factor → development and differentiation.
  • Wnt/β-Catenin: Wnt ligand inhibits degradation of β-catenin → β-catenin enters nucleus → transcription of proliferative genes. Critical in colon cancer.

Adaptor Proteins & Signal Amplification

Adaptor proteins have multiple domains (SH2, SH3) that mediate protein-protein interactions — they are the "connectors" of signaling cascades. Key concept:

🔑 Signal Amplification One receptor activation event can trigger phosphorylation of many downstream proteins → exponential amplification of the initial signal through cascades of kinase activity.

Growth Factors & Receptors

// Module 12 · Proliferation & Repair

What Are Growth Factors?

Growth factors stimulate the survival and proliferation of particular cells, as well as differentiation, migration, and other cell activities. They bind to specific cell surface receptors and influence the cell cycle.

🔑 Important Principle Growth factors don't just cause proliferation — they can also promote entry into the cell cycle, enhance biosynthesis of cellular components, and enhance survival. Some are "competence" factors; others are "progression" factors.

Major Growth Factors in Regeneration & Repair

GROWTH FACTOR SOURCES MAIN FUNCTIONS
EGF / TGF-α Macrophages, epithelial cells Mitogenic for epithelial cells, hepatocytes, fibroblasts
HGF (Hepatocyte GF) Fibroblasts, liver stromal cells Hepatocyte proliferation; promotes epithelial growth & migration
VEGF Mesenchymal cells Angiogenesis; increases vascular permeability; critical in tumors
PDGF Platelets, macrophages, endothelium Fibroblast migration/proliferation; smooth muscle activation
FGF (1, 2, 7) Macrophages, mast cells, endothelium Angiogenesis; wound healing; keratinocyte growth (KGF=FGF7)
TGF-β T lymphocytes, macrophages, platelets Fibroblast/smooth muscle stimulation; ECM synthesis; immunosuppression

Receptor Tyrosine Kinases

EGF receptor (EGFR/ERBB2) family are the most important RTKs. ERBB2 is highly amplified in a subset of breast cancers — targeted by trastuzumab (Herceptin).

  • Ligand binding → dimerization → autophosphorylation
  • Downstream: RAS, PI3K, MAPK pathways
  • Gain-of-function mutations: proto-oncogenes → oncogenes

Transcription Factors

The ultimate targets of signaling cascades. They bind DNA and regulate gene expression. Most signal transduction pathways ultimately influence cellular gene expression. Key TFs:

  • MYC — proliferation (amplified in many cancers)
  • p53 — tumor suppressor; "guardian of the genome"
  • NF-κB — inflammation and immunity
🚨 Oncogene vs. Tumor Suppressor Gain-of-function mutations in growth factor genes or receptors create proto-oncogenes → oncogenes (cancer-promoting). Loss-of-function mutations in negative regulators eliminate tumor suppression.
